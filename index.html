This project explores New Zealand’s love for cats and dogs through an interactive web-based visualisation.

We aim to reveal not just ownership trends, but also how companion animals shape people’s lifestyles, communities, and daily choices.

The visualisation moves from macro to micro, starting with national ownership rates and geographic differences, and drilling down to pet acquisition methods, ownership motivations, demographic profiles such as the age of owners, and barriers faced by future pet owners.

Our primary dataset is the Companion Animals New Zealand Pet Data Report 2024, based on a national survey conducted by Companion Animals NZ.

This graph presents the regional distribution of cat and dog ownership in New Zealand, based on household survey data.

Households in the Rest of the North Island are the most likely to own a dog. (38%). In contrast, Auckland has the lowest dog ownership rate (24%), which is significantly lower than other regions. Cat ownership follows a similar pattern.

Specifically, rest of the North Island also leads in cat ownership, with 46% of households owning a cat, while Auckland again shows the lowest ownership rate at 35%.

Meanwhile, households in the Rest of the South Island report equal ownership rates for cats and dogs, both at 36%.

The sample sizes for each region vary and may influence the statistical reliability of the pet ownership rates presented. In particular, regions with larger base sizes—such as Rest of the North Island (n=666) and Auckland (n=627)—provide stronger confidence in the reported trends.

However, regions with smaller sample sizes (such as Rest of South Island and Canterbury) may have wider margins of error, and their reported percentages should be interpreted with caution.

Cats remain the most popular companion animal (40% of households) and over the past 4 years. There is no significant change in the cat population. It is estimated that there are approximately 1.26 million companion cats.

The number of households who share their household with a dog has declined from 34% in 2020 to 31% in 2024. There are an estimated 830,000 companion dogs in New Zealand.

The ways in which New Zealanders acquire cats and dogs differ notably. Cats are most commonly obtained through animal rescue organisations, particularly the SPCA (22%). 16% of cats were found or adopted as strays, compared to just 1% of dogs, both suggesting cats are more commonly acquired informally or opportunistically.

While dogs are most frequently acquired from breeders (34%) or via private advertisements (21%), indicating more formal and commercial acquisition patterns. Informal networks (e.g. friends, family, neighbours) also play a key role in both cat and dog acquisition.

Among households without a companion animal, the top reasons for not wanting one are the perceived cost (48% of respondents) and time commitments (49% of respondents). Also mentioned were concerns about responsibility (36% of respondents). About 29% of respondents cited messiness (such as fur or dirt) as a reason, while 20% avoided pets due to the sadness they might experience when the animal passes away.

These reasons highlight that emotional and sensory sensitivities—rather than purely logistical constraints—also play a significant role in pet ownership decisions.

Based on a national survey, the Companion Animals New Zealand (CANZ) Pet Data Report 2024 is the only representative and independent source of pet ownership insights in New Zealand.

To construct a clear and engaging narrative, we curated six key sections that guide the viewer from broad context to detailed insights. With vibrant colors, playful illustrations, and interactive elements, each data story offers a fun and cohesive experience—inviting users to explore and discover the joy of data.

We sincerely thank Companion Animals New Zealand for their valuable report, which provided high-level insights aligned with our project goals. Their work continues to enrich our understanding of animal welfare through reliable data and perspectives.
