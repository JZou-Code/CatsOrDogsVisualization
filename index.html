When we feel lonely, who stays with us first？

Is it our friends？ Our family？ For many people in New Zealand, the answer is a warm, furry companion.

In this country of 5.31 million people, there are over 1.26 million cats and 850,000 dogs. They’re not just pets, but companions, emotional supporters, even family.

So who loves cats more？ And who prefers dogs？

This question may sound casual, even like a joke. But behind it lies a quiet map of human behavior -— age, city, lifestyle... every “cat or dog” choice reflects how we live and connect.

That’s why we ask: can we use data to understand love？

Maybe not fully -— but we can trace its shape. Behind every data point is a real household, a real connection, from the Animals in New Zealand report, which surveyed 2,058 households.

We have a gentle wish: to turn cold data into something visible and tender. To tell quiet stories of how cats and dogs bring warmth -— and teach us how to love.

In New Zealand, the highest rates of pet ownership aren’t found in the busy cities —- not even in Auckland. Instead, they appear in the quieter, often overlooked parts of the North Island.

In the “Rest of the North Island,” 46% of households own cats—the highest rate in the country.

In contrast, Auckland’s rates are much lower: 35% for cats and just 24% for dogs.

In the South Island’s quieter areas, cat and dog ownership is even—both at 36%.

These insights come from a national survey of 2,058 households, including 666 from the Rest of the North Island and 627 from Auckland —- large enough to reflect reliable trends.

Maybe we don’t love animals any less in the cities —- but as life gets faster and more crowded, we may quietly drift away from gentleness.

Cat ownership in New Zealand has remained steady over the past four years—about 40% of households, or 1.26 million cats. Like quiet companions, they stay—neither increasing nor fading.

Dog ownership, however, has declined from 34% in 2020 to 31% in 2024, with an estimated 830,000 dogs. A small drop on paper, but behind it may be stories of people who wanted a dog -— but faced busy lives, limited space, or the quiet fear of not being able to care enough.

This chart shows how differently we meet the cats and dogs in our lives. Cats often feel like chance encounters -— 22% come from SPCA, and 16% were found as strays. Many aren’t purchased, but arrive through friends, neighbors, or simply appear —- quietly becoming part of the home. Dogs, on the other hand, are usually the result of a clear decision: 34% come from breeders, and 21% through advertisements -— more formal, intentional beginnings.

One feels like fate, the other, a choice. But no matter how they arrive, they end up in the same place -— with us.

People aged 45 to 54 have the highest pet ownership—52% for cats, 34% for dogs.

At this stage, some are raising children and want a gentle companion at home.

Others have seen their children grow up and leave, and pets become the new source of comfort.

Some live alone and choose a quiet, loyal presence for themselves.

Cat ownership increases with age, peaking between 45 and 64 (48–52%), then declines after 65. Dog ownership is more steady across age groups, but also drops among seniors.

As life changes, so do the roles pets play, and the people most likely to have them are often those who’ve learned to treasure stillness and loyalty.

The most common reason people gave for getting a cat or dog was simple: companionship. Whether it’s a cat curling up by the window or a dog greeting you at the door —- what people long for is connection. Another frequent reason was: “I’ve had this kind of pet before.” Behind that answer is something quietly powerful -— some people aren’t looking for something new, but something familiar. A reminder of the one that once kept them company through childhood, loneliness, or love.

This shows that choosing a pet is not just a practical decision —- it’s often rooted in memory, emotion, and the kind of bond that’s hard to explain but deeply felt.

This chart shows more than just barriers -— it reveals gentle reasons behind hesitation. Many people don’t reject pets out of unwillingness, but because life feels too full: 49% said they don’t have time, 48% cited cost, and 36% felt it’s too much responsibility. Most touching of all — 1 in 5 said they fear the heartbreak of losing them. It’s not a rejection of love, it’s the ache of imagining the goodbye, before the love even begins.

These reasons highlight that emotional and sensory sensitivities —- rather than purely logistical constraints -— also play a significant role in pet ownership decisions.

Our project is based on the 2024 Pet Data Report by Companion Animals New Zealand (CANZ), a nationally representative survey of pet ownership. We selected key variables -— such as pet types, sourcing, ownership by region and age -— and turned them into six themed visual sections that tell the story of cats and dogs in Aotearoa.

To make the data more accessible and engaging, we used hand-drawn visual styles, playful layouts, and light interactive effects. We matched each dataset with the most suitable chart type—ranging from icon-based summaries to flow maps and radial plots. Our goal was to turn cold numbers into a warm, friendly experience for animal lovers.

Data source: Companion Animals New Zealand (CANZ) – 2024 Pet Data Report. Special thanks to CANZ for making this data publicly available.
